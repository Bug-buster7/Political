The Cold War: How Ideology Became Geopolitics

From 1947 to 1991, two superpowers — the United States and Soviet Union — redrew the entire map of global power without ever firing a single direct shot at each other. This case explores how ideological competition (capitalism vs. communism) shaped alliances, triggered proxy wars, and created a nuclear standoff that held the world in tension for 44 years.

Essential Books for Every Political Mind

Before you can shape policy, you must understand the language politics was built on. These four texts are the non-negotiable foundation.

🏛️
~380 BC

The Republic

Plato

The original blueprint for political philosophy. Explores justice, the ideal state, and the philosopher-king. Every modern political argument traces roots here.

♟️
1532

The Prince

Niccolò Machiavelli

The first realist text in political science. Machiavelli separated politics from morality — still controversial, still essential for understanding power.

🐋
1651

Leviathan

Thomas Hobbes

The foundation of social contract theory. Without a sovereign state, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." The basis of modern state legitimacy.

M. Laxmikanth

The definitive guide to India's constitutional and political system. Essential for understanding the world's largest democracy — 1.4 billion citizens governed under one constitution.

Ethics & Logic Wing

Think Sharper. Decide Wiser.

Politics is 20% knowledge and 80% judgment. Understanding logical fallacies and ethical dilemmas is what separates a politician from a statesman.

🧠 Common Logical Fallacies in Political Debate

Ad Hominem
Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. Common in political debates and campaign rhetoric.
Straw Man
Misrepresenting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack. Often appears in legislative debate and policy criticism.
False Dilemma
Presenting only two options when more exist. "You are either with us or against us" is the classic political form.
Slippery Slope
Claiming one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without evidence. Frequently used to oppose policy reforms.
Appeal to Authority
Using the endorsement of a notable figure as a substitute for actual evidence in an argument.

⚖️ Political Ethics: Hard Choices in Power

Great leaders don't just make popular decisions — they make difficult ones. These case studies in political ethics have no clean answers, only better-informed judgment.

The Problem of Dirty Hands
Can a leader commit a morally wrong act for the greater political good? Philosopher Michael Walzer's classic dilemma: if torturing one person saves a thousand, what does a leader do?
Whistleblowing and Loyalty
When a government official discovers wrongdoing, does their duty to the public override their institutional loyalty? The Ellsberg and Snowden cases offer contrasting answers.
Trolley Problem in Governance
How should leaders triage suffering? Utilitarian vs. deontological ethics — and why policy-making often forces leaders to choose between two harms, not a harm and a good.
